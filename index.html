Floods, Rainfall and Losses Across Malaysia

Floods are one of Malaysia’s most frequent natural hazards, often linked to seasonal rainfall patterns. This dashboard provides an overview of flood occurrences across Malaysian states from 2000 to 2024. The map displays relative flood frequency per state, normalized against the state with the highest flood count. Click on a state to explore its monthly rainfall for the selected year, helping you understand how rainfall patterns relate to flood events.

Rainfall Variability

Malaysia’s rainfall patterns vary widely across regions due to differing exposure to the Northeast Monsoon. The box plot visualizes the distribution of daily rainfall (mm) across states, highlighting how Monsoon-Affected regions generally experience higher rainfall medians and wider variability compared to less affected states. The broader interquartile ranges for eastern coastal states suggest greater day-to-day rainfall fluctuations, aligning with seasonal monsoon impacts.

Flood Conservation and Emergency Losses

This grouped stacked bar chart compares the flood emergency and conservation losses across Malaysian states in 2022 and 2023.

From the chart, it is clear that overall losses decreased significantly from 2022 to 2023 in most states. For example, Selangor recorded a sharp drop from over RM69,000 in total losses in 2022 to only about RM7,957 in 2023. Similarly, states like Kelantan, Sabah, and Pahang also saw noticeable declines, suggesting improved flood control measures or fewer severe flood events in 2023.

However, not all states followed this trend. Perak experienced a notable increase in total loss, rising from around RM18,199 in 2022 to RM26,358 in 2023, mainly driven by higher emergency losses. This could indicate localised flood incidents or new vulnerabilities emerging in 2023.

In terms of loss composition, most states in 2022 had higher conservation losses compared to emergency losses, meaning more resources were allocated to recovery and preventive measures. In 2023, this balance shifted towards emergency losses in several states, suggesting that while overall damage was reduced, immediate response costs remained relatively high in certain regions.

Overall, the chart highlights an encouraging pattern, nationwide flood impacts are declining, but continued attention is needed in states to sustain this improvement. The data also emphasises the importance of maintaining effective flood conservation programs even during years of lower flood activity.
